Research Data Managment

Oct 23, 2024

Workshop

This workshop on research data management in the life sciences was developed at the University of Vienna and delivered in an interactive format using LiaScript. It introduced the principles and practices of handling research data across the entire research cycle, with the goal of making data FAIR: Findable, Accessible, Interoperable, and Reusable. Rather than treating data management as an afterthought, the course framed it as a foundation of good scientific practice. Each module combined theory, case studies, and practical exercises to highlight both the opportunities and the responsibilities that come with managing research data in the life sciences.

Take aways

We came away with a clearer sense of how data management supports reproducibility, collaboration, and long-term impact. The course showed us that good RDM reduces risks of data loss, makes results easier to verify, and opens the door for reuse by other researchers. What might seem like administrative work at first proved to be deeply tied to research quality and visibility. The main takeaway was that RDM is not a bureaucratic hurdle but a practical tool that strengthens science when it is built into projects from the very beginning.

Core Topics

The course began by establishing why research data management matters in the life sciences. Good practices were linked directly to reproducibility, transparency, and long-term value of research projects. Instead of seeing data as something produced only for immediate analysis, we were encouraged to view it as a resource with a lifecycle that extends from project planning to archiving and reuse.

One major focus was planning. We explored how to create a data management plan that anticipates what types of data will be generated, how they will be documented, and where they will be stored. This planning stage was presented as crucial for meeting funder requirements, protecting sensitive data, and avoiding later confusion.

Documentation and metadata formed another central theme. We looked at how consistent file formats, standardized vocabularies, and rich metadata make datasets easier to interpret long after the original project has ended. Examples showed how poorly documented data quickly loses its value, while well-structured documentation makes it possible for other researchers to build on previous work.

Storage, backup, and security were also treated as fundamental. The course emphasized strategies to avoid data loss, ranging from multiple backup systems to secure servers for sensitive material. Finally, we examined the principles and practices of data sharing, including the use of repositories, the role of licensing, and the importance of FAIR principles in ensuring that shared data can actually be discovered and reused.

Practical Skills

Alongside the conceptual frameworks, the course placed strong emphasis on practical habits that make research data easier to manage on a daily basis. File naming conventions and folder structures were presented as deceptively simple tools that prevent confusion and wasted time. By adopting clear, consistent naming patterns, it becomes possible to immediately recognize the contents and versions of files without relying on memory or ad hoc notes. Folder hierarchies were shown as a way to mirror project logic, separating raw data, processed data, scripts, and outputs.

We also practiced writing data management plans. These documents translate general principles into concrete steps, defining how data will be handled at every stage of a project. They serve both as planning instruments for researchers and as accountability tools for funders and institutions. Drafting them required us to think ahead about file formats, documentation standards, storage solutions, and sharing strategies.

Version control was another key skill introduced in the course. Tools such as Git were discussed as powerful methods to track changes, collaborate across teams, and ensure that previous states of code or data can always be recovered. This aspect of RDM linked directly to reproducibility, since version control provides a transparent record of how analyses evolve.

Finally, we learned how to select appropriate repositories and apply licenses for data reuse. Repositories ensure long-term accessibility and visibility of research outputs, while licenses provide legal clarity about how others may use the data. Together, these practices transform individual projects into contributions to the wider scientific community, making it possible for results to be validated, reused, and extended.

Ethics and Standards

The course also addressed the ethical and legal dimensions of research data management. Protecting sensitive information was a recurring concern, especially in cases involving personal data, medical records, or endangered species locations. We examined strategies for anonymization, controlled access, and secure storage, all of which ensure that data can be useful without compromising privacy or safety.

Ethical considerations extended beyond privacy. We discussed questions of ownership and responsibility, including how credit should be assigned when data is reused and how to handle collaborations across institutions or countries. The course highlighted that ethical data management is not just about compliance but about fairness, respect, and accountability in the research process.

Legal frameworks were presented as another layer researchers must navigate. National and institutional guidelines, as well as international regulations, determine what can and cannot be shared. Funder requirements add a further dimension, often mandating open access or specific repository use. Learning to interpret these frameworks was shown to be as essential as the technical skills of storing or formatting data.

By situating data management within this ethical and legal context, the course underscored that RDM is not only a technical challenge but also a moral and professional responsibility. Decisions about storage, sharing, and reuse always involve values, and awareness of these dimensions is crucial for responsible scientific practice.

Licensing

A dedicated part of the course focused on licensing, showing how legal frameworks shape the reuse of data, software, and other research outputs. Instead of treating licenses as abstract legal texts, we worked through practical scenarios that mirrored real situations in scientific projects. For example, we examined what happens when code is reused from GitHub repositories, when datasets are shared with conditions on attribution or commercialization, or when educational materials are made freely available for teaching.

These scenarios highlighted the importance of choosing the right license for each type of output. For software, permissive licenses such as MIT or more protective ones such as GPL determine how others can modify and redistribute code. For datasets, Creative Commons licenses provide a spectrum of options, ranging from CC-BY, which requires attribution, to more restrictive forms that limit reuse or prevent commercial exploitation. We also explored licensing strategies for teaching resources and even equipment designs, extending the discussion beyond data and software into broader areas of research output.

The exercises showed how licenses clarify expectations for both authors and users. A well-chosen license ensures proper credit, protects against misuse, and encourages reuse by reducing legal uncertainty. We also discussed the risks of neglecting licensing, such as creating barriers for collaboration or unintentionally restricting visibility.

The overarching lesson was that licensing is not an afterthought but part of planning for impact. By assigning clear licenses to code, data, or materials, researchers not only protect their own work but also enable others to build on it. This makes licensing a crucial link between individual projects and the wider scientific community.
