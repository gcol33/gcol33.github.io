Lecture 2 – Introduction to Spatial Models

This lecture introduced the rationale and mechanics behind incorporating spatial structure into ecological models, particularly occupancy and abundance models. At the heart of this discussion lies a simple but powerful observation: most ecological processes are spatial. Species distributions are not randomly scattered; they are shaped by underlying spatial dependencies. This is not a minor detail but a central feature of ecological systems, and spatial models aim to incorporate it explicitly. The lecture began with a key concept from geography: Tobler’s First Law:

Everything is related to everything else, but near things are more related than distant things. 

Spatial autocorrelation is a formal expression of this idea. If nearby sites tend to resemble each other, then traditional models that assume independent residuals are misspecified. Ignoring spatial autocorrelation can lead to biased inference, overly optimistic uncertainty estimates, and poor predictive performance.

We reviewed three types of spatial data: point-referenced, areal, and point pattern. Point-referenced data are the focus here, where each observation has a known coordinate (such as latitude and longitude). These data are sampled from a continuous spatial surface and are common in ecological studies—for example, when recording tree measurements, species detections, or environmental variables at survey sites.

Although areal models (like conditional autoregressive (CAR) or simultaneous autoregressive (SAR) models) are often used for spatial analyses, the lecture argued that point-referenced models offer more flexibility, especially for large datasets or when prediction surfaces are needed. Moreover, animal movement introduces ambiguity in the effective sampling area, further justifying the use of continuous-space models.

To motivate spatial modeling, we examined data from a forest stand in Oregon, where 600 trees had been surveyed. A linear model was used to predict tree diameter at breast height (DBH) based on species identity. Residual plots showed clear spatial patterns: neighboring trees tended to have similar residuals. This violated the assumption of independent and identically distributed residuals and pointed to missing spatial structure in the model.

To quantify spatial autocorrelation more formally, we turned to the semivariogram. For any pair of locations \( s_j \) and \( s_k \), the empirical semivariogram measures the average squared difference in response values as a function of distance. A rising semivariogram indicates spatial correlation: points close together have more similar values than those further apart. A flat semivariogram would suggest no spatial structure. In the tree data, the semivariogram rose clearly with distance, confirming that spatial autocorrelation was present and that a spatial model was warranted.

So, what is a spatial model? In this context, it is a model that includes a spatially structured random effect \( w(s) \), which captures residual spatial variation not explained by covariates. The goal is to model this surface and use it to improve predictions, explain variation, and reduce bias. Importantly, this spatial effect is assumed to be a smooth surface over geographic space.

The lecture introduced Gaussian Processes (GPs) as the gold standard for spatial modeling. A GP is a stochastic process that defines a distribution over functions. Any finite collection of locations yields a multivariate normal distribution. GPs are fully specified by a mean function (often assumed to be zero) and a covariance function, which describes how the values at two locations are correlated as a function of distance.

This covariance is determined by a spatial covariance matrix, where the entries depend on both the distance between sites and the choice of covariance function. The lecture focused on isotropic covariance functions, where correlation depends only on distance and not on direction. These functions are easier to interpret and estimate than anisotropic functions.

Several covariance functions were discussed, including exponential, spherical, Gaussian, and Matérn. The exponential covariance function was emphasized for its simplicity and wide use in ecological modeling. It is defined as

\[\text{Cov}(w(s_j), w(s_k)) = \sigma^2 \exp(-\phi \, d_{jk}),\]

where \( \sigma^2 \) is the spatial variance, \( \phi \) is the spatial decay parameter, and \( d_{jk} \) is the Euclidean distance between sites \( s_j \) and \( s_k \). The decay parameter \( \phi \) controls how rapidly correlation declines with distance. The effective spatial range the distance at which spatial correlation drops below 0.05, is approximately \( 3/\phi \).

Covariance functions are inversely related to semivariograms. A rapidly decaying covariance implies a rapidly rising semivariogram, and vice versa. In practice, these functions allow us to capture and visualize the smooth spatial surfaces that underlie observed patterns. GPs also make prediction easy, because the joint distribution of observed and new sites remains multivariate normal.

This process of spatial prediction is known as kriging in frequentist statistics, and in Bayesian models, it arises naturally from the posterior predictive distribution. Because the spatial surface is continuous, we can estimate values at unsampled locations and generate smooth prediction maps: Critical for conservation applications, species distribution modeling, and ecological forecasting.

In terms of estimation, the lecture advocated strongly for the Bayesian approach. While frequentist methods like maximum likelihood or REML are possible, they tend to underestimate uncertainty in the spatial parameters \( \sigma^2 \) and \( \phi \). Bayesian models, in contrast, provide full posterior distributions and allow for direct modeling of uncertainty.

Bayesian spatial models assign priors to all parameters, including the spatial variance and decay. In the `spOccupancy` package, the default priors are designed to be mildly informative. For example, the prior for \( \phi \) is uniform over a range corresponding to the minimum and maximum inter-site distances in the dataset, allowing both short- and long-range spatial structure. The spatial variance \( \sigma^2 \) typically receives an inverse-gamma prior, with mean 1 and infinite variance when the shape parameter is 2.

Despite their advantages, spatial models come with challenges. They are computationally more intensive, sometimes harder to fit, and require more data to reliably estimate spatial parameters. They are also vulnerable to spatial confounding, a situation where covariates are correlated with the spatial random effect. This can distort estimates and make it difficult to interpret regression coefficients.

To diagnose spatial confounding, several strategies were proposed. One is to compare the spatial random effect with covariates directly. If they are strongly correlated, confounding is likely. Another is to fit both spatial and non-spatial models and examine whether the covariate estimates differ markedly. If they do, interpretation becomes more complicated. Using more informative priors on spatial parameters may help reduce confounding, although the benefits are still debated.

The session concluded with a practical exercise using the Western Experimental Forest tree data. By modeling DBH with both species identity and spatial random effects, participants were able to produce better predictions and visualize spatial patterns. These visualizations help uncover ecological processes not captured by measured covariates and suggest new hypotheses about local variation. Ultimately, spatial models expand the realism of occupancy modeling. They acknowledge that nearby sites are not independent, and they let us consider that structure for more accurate inference.
