## The photo

This was taken on the black sand beach at Jökulsárlón, Iceland — a stretch of shore where icebergs calved from the Breiðamerkurjökull glacier wash up, melt for a few hours, and disappear back into the sea. The piece of ice in the foreground had probably been on the beach for less than a day when I photographed it. The black sand is volcanic basalt. The ocean behind it is the North Atlantic.

What struck me was the contrast: something ancient — glacier ice that may have been compressed for centuries — sitting on something equally ancient, slowly returning to water. The whole scene lasts a few hours, then it is gone.

## Why I submitted it

The [Life Sciences Faculty photo contest](https://lifesciences.univie.ac.at/innovation-impact/photo-contest/) asked for images that connect to research themes in life sciences. My research looks at how species distributions shift under global change — how life reorganizes across space and time as climate, land use, and disturbance reshape ecosystems.

Glacier retreat is one of the clearest signals of that reorganization. The beach at Jökulsárlón did not look like this fifty years ago. The glacier has retreated several kilometres since the 1930s, and the lagoon it created — now the largest in Iceland — grows every decade. What reads as a beautiful, otherworldly landscape is also a record of change.

The submission description:

> In the age of the Anthropocene, even small coastal scenes echo the planetary transformations reshaping ecosystems. My research examines these shifting patterns as life reorganizes across space and time.
